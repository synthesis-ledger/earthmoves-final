## Origins of the Kp Index

Solar wind sculpts Earth's magnetosphere daily. Geomagnetic storms pulse in response. Julius Bartels forged the Kp index in 1939. He drew from 13 observatories in Europe and North America. These stations, spaced for global reach, tracked horizontal field shifts in nanoteslas.

Each site yielded a local K-value, scaled quasi-logarithmically from 0 to 9. Bartels weighted them into planetary Kp. Regional noise smoothed out. Today, GFZ in Potsdam crunches magnetometer traces from the same 13: Niemegk, Eskdalemuir, Sitka, Canberra. Releases hit every three hours: 0000, 0300 UTC onward.

NOAA's Space Weather Prediction Center nowcasts and forecasts 1—4 days ahead. DSCOVR at L1 feeds satellite data. Ground arrays refine it. Kp gauges the ring current at 3—5 Earth radii. CMEs or coronal hole streams swell it. Mild CMEs lift Kp to 4. Carrington events spike to 9.

Swarm satellites, orbiting at 450 km since 2013, affirm the scale's hold over 80 years.

## Decoding the Kp Scale and Its Drivers

Kp steps from 0+ to 9– in thirds. Each triples quiet-day variations. Kp 0 holds below 5 nT/hour. Kp 9 exceeds 500 nT/hour. Quiet Kp 0—3 mirrors 300—400 km/s solar wind, density 5 cm⁻³.

Storms at Kp 4—6 demand speeds over 500 km/s or Bz to –10 nT. Kp 7—8 needs dense plasma, Bz below –20 nT. Kp 9 distorts the magnetosphere. Satellites drag. Grids falter.

Solar wind protons and electrons race at 10⁶ km/h. They etch the auroral oval at 65—75°N under Kp 3. Higher Kp swells it equatorward. keV electrons plunge. Oxygen glows green at 100—150 km (557.7 nm). Nitrogen flares purple (391.4 nm) or red (630 nm).
