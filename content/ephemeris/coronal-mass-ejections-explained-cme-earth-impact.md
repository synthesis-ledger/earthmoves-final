# Coronal Mass Ejections Explained: When the Sun Hurls Billions of Tons at Earth

Coronal mass ejections hurl solar plasma and magnetic flux from the Sun's corona. They carry 1-10 billion metric tons of material. Speeds range from 250 km/s to 3,000 km/s. Earth feels the impact in 15-80 hours. The blow compresses our magnetosphere's dayside from 10 Earth radii to 6, igniting NOAA G1-to-G5 storms.

These storms push auroras equatorward. High-frequency radio blacks out. GPS drifts tens of meters. Satellites drag through thickened air. Geomagnetically induced currents overload transformers—like Quebec's 1989 blackout.

## What Is a Coronal Mass Ejection?

Twisted clouds of ionized gas erupt: protons, electrons, heavier ions laced with magnetic fields. These fields stretch billions of times weaker than a fridge magnet, yet span continents on the Sun. OSO-7 first spotted them in 1971. They birth above active regions near sunspots, where fields tangle.

Earth-bound CMEs wield southward fields. These reconnect with our northward magnetosphere. Energy surges into ionosphere and ring current. Van Allen belts disrupt. Particles accelerate into auroral rain; fields flicker into our grids.

Slower CMEs, under 500 km/s, blend into solar wind as magnetic clouds. Faster ones plow shocks ahead, spiking radiation for astronauts. A dense plasma flux rope twists at the core—helical magnetic bundle. Expanding flanks sheath it; supersonic leads birth a shock.
