# Aurora Borealis & Australis Explained: Complete Guide to Causes, Colours & Viewing Tips

Solar wind hurls charged particles from the Sun at 400-700 km/s. Earth's magnetosphere funnels them to the poles. Oxygen ignites green glows at 100 km altitude, red above 200 km. Nitrogen sparks blue and purple from 50-150 km. Hunt them at 60°+ N/S latitudes. Dark skies. Local midnight. Kp index at 3 or higher.

## The Science Behind Aurora Borealis and Australis

Solar wind blasts protons and electrons toward Earth. This plasma arrives in 2-5 days, laced with magnetic fields. Earth's magnetosphere—a comet-tailed shield—stretches 60,000 km sunward, millions tailward. It deflects most onslaughts. Coronal mass ejections pierce the dayside magnetopause through reconnection. Particles flood in.

Electrons spiral down field lines into the auroral oval—a ring at 67° magnetic latitude, both hemispheres. Storms push it equatorward. Particles hit 10,000-100,000 km/s, plunging 50-500 km deep. They rip electrons from oxygen and nitrogen. Recombining electrons release photons: oxygen at 557.7 nm green, 630.0 nm red. Nitrogen flares 391.4 nm blue-violet.

Kp index tracks the chaos—0 quiet, 9 extreme. Kp 3 draws faint arcs at 65°. Kp 5 reaches 50°. Solar cycle 25 peaks now, 2024-2025, lighting polar skies near-daily. Equatorial flashes demand Kp 8+, like 2003's rare show.

The Sun's 11-year dynamo pulses link to our nights. Molten iron churns 3,000 km below, forging the field that guards our air—and sculpts cosmic fire into light.

## Decoding the Colours of the Aurora

Oxygen at 100-150 km births green. Its ¹S → ¹D shift at 557.7 nm glows after a one-second pause—diffuse, alive. Red rules above 200 km: 630.0 nm from ¹D → ³S, lingering where collisions thin.
