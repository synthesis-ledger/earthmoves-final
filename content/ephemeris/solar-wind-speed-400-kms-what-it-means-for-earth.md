# Solar Wind Speed Explained: What 400 km/s Means for Earth

## What 400 km/s Solar Wind Means for Earth

Solar wind at 400 km/s drifts in like a steady breeze. Protons, electrons, and alpha particles fill space at 5-10 per cubic centimetre. The corona's million-kelvin heat drives this supersonic rush past 100-200 km/s sound speeds.

Ram pressure \( P = \rho v^2 \) hits 1.3 nPa. Density \(\rho \approx 8 \times 10^{-21}\) kg/m³ indents the magnetosphere from 10 to 8-9 Earth radii. Particles stream into polar cusps. They energise 1-10 keV electrons. Oxygen and nitrogen glow faint green (557.7 nm) and red (630 nm) over Thule or Alert.

In Tromsø, arcs pulse 10-20% brighter. Fjords catch shadows from 100-200 km altitudes. No southward Bz means reconnection stays below 10¹⁴ W. Geostationary satellites see voltages under 100 V.

Earth's shield holds quiet. Billions of years tune its balance. 400 km/s whispers across the void.

## The Solar Wind's Journey to Earth

Solar wind crosses 1 AU: 149,597,871 km. At 400 km/s, it takes 374,495 seconds—4.33 days. Particles spiral from solar limbs, traced by 1-10 MeV/nucleon ions. The Sun's 27-day rotation modulates the stream.

Coronal holes birth faster flows: 600-800 km/s. A 700 km/s stream needs 2.47 days; 800 km/s, 2.16. These rivers compress slower wind ahead to 20 particles/cc.
